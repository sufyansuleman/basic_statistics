Welcome

This course teaches statistics the way research actually works starting with your data, not a textbook formula. Every method is introduced with a concrete lab scenario, explained in plain language, and then demonstrated step-by-step in R.

The course is designed for PhD students in biomedical and clinical research. That includes people working with human patient data, mouse and animal experiments, genomics, survey data, or longitudinal studies. You do not need to be an expert in R or statistics to start but you do need R and RStudio installed on your computer.

Start Here: Install R and Course Packages

Before opening any session, go to Before You Begin: Setup and follow the four steps there. You will install R, RStudio, and all the packages the course uses. This takes about 10–15 minutes and only needs to be done once.

How the Course Is Organized

The course has six parts. Each part builds on the previous one.

Choose Your Starting Point
Your background Where to start
New to R and statistics Part 1 work through everything in order
Know R basics, new to statistics Part 2 skip Part 1
Comfortable with R and data visualization Part 3 skip Parts 1 and 2
Here only for statistical concepts (not learning R) Part 3 skip Parts 1 and 2; read the narrative, ignore the code

Parts 1 and 2 are R and data skills. The statistics starts at Part 3.

Your Quick Reference

The Statistical Decision Guide in Part 6 is a reference card you can return to at any point. It tells you which method to use for any research question, with links back to the relevant session. Bookmark it now.

Part 1: R Foundations

Already comfortable with R? If you can install packages, work with data frames, and load a CSV file, skip Part 1 and go straight to Part 2. These sessions are here for students who are new to R or need a quick refresh.

  • R packages and libraries what they are and how to install them
  • Vectors, data frames, and factors the building blocks of R data
  • Importing, reshaping, and cleaning data with dplyr and tidyr

Part 2: Describing and Visualizing Data

Always look at your data before running any test. Part 2 teaches you to summarize and visualize data so you understand its structure, spot problems, and communicate findings clearly.

  • Descriptive statistics: means, medians, distributions, missing data
  • Basic visualization with ggplot2
  • Advanced visualization for publications and presentations
  • Exploring real clinical datasets end-to-end

Part 3: Inference Testing and Estimation

This is the conceptual heart of statistics. What does a p-value actually mean? When is a result real versus noise? How many samples do you need? Part 3 answers these questions for the most common testing situations.

  • Sampling, confidence intervals, and the central limit theorem
  • Hypothesis testing: the full framework p-values, effect sizes, Type I/II errors
  • t-tests for comparing groups
  • Non-parametric tests for when your data violates assumptions
  • ANOVA for comparing three or more groups
  • Categorical data: chi-square and Fisher’s exact tests
  • Power and sample size: how to design a well-powered study

Part 4: Regression and Modeling

Regression is how you predict outcomes and understand relationships between variables. Part 4 covers the full modeling cycle fit, diagnose, interpret, communicate for the most common model types in biomedical research.

  • Correlation and association
  • Linear regression predicting continuous outcomes
  • Multiple regression multiple predictors and confounding
  • Logistic regression predicting binary outcomes (yes/no)
  • Model building and diagnostics how to check if your model is any good
  • Handling missing data with multiple imputation
  • Mixed models for repeated measurements and clustered data

Part 5: Advanced Methods

Part 5 covers methods that are increasingly important in PhD-level research but are rarely taught in introductory statistics courses. These sessions assume you have completed Parts 1–4.

  • Survival analysis and time-to-event data
  • Causal inference: DAGs, confounders, and observational studies
  • Mendelian randomization: using genetics to infer causality
  • Statistical concepts in omics and high-dimensional data

Part 6: Reference and Resources

The final part is not new statistical methods it is tools for thinking and tools for your research career.

  • Statistical Decision Guide a complete “which test when” reference
  • Clinical research methods study designs and their statistical implications
  • Help and documentation how to find answers when you are stuck

Prerequisites

  • R and RStudio installed on your computer
  • No prior statistics knowledge is assumed beyond high-school level
  • Basic ability to run R code in RStudio (if you cannot do this yet, start with Part 1, Session 1)

How to Use This Course

Each session follows the same structure:

  1. When do you use this? A concrete scenario from your field
  2. Background Why the method works, in plain language
  3. Worked examples Step-by-step R code on real datasets
  4. What can go wrong Common mistakes and how to avoid them
  5. Exercises Practice problems with solutions
  6. Comprehension check Self-check quiz questions

You can work through the course week by week, or jump to a specific method when you need it. If you are unsure which method to use, go to the Statistical Decision Guide in Part 6.
