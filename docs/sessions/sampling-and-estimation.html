9  Sampling, Estimation, and Confidence Intervals

9.1 When Do You Use This?

You have collected a sample and want to make inferences about the broader population it came from � not just describe the sample itself. Estimation produces a best guess (point estimate) for a population quantity along with a confidence interval that quantifies how uncertain that guess is given your sample size. For example: estimating the mean level of a biomarker in all patients with a condition from a clinic sample, or the proportion who respond to treatment in a trial.

9.2 Learning Objectives

After completing this session you will be able to:

  • Explain what a sampling distribution and standard error are
  • State the Central Limit Theorem and explain why it matters for inference
  • Compute confidence intervals for means and proportions in R
  • Interpret a 95% confidence interval correctly
  • Distinguish between a confidence interval and a prediction interval
  • Use bootstrap resampling when distributional assumptions are uncertain

9.3 Background: From Sample to Population

You can never measure every person, mouse, or cell. You measure a sample and use it to estimate a population quantity. Three ideas underpin all of this:

Point estimate: Your best single guess at the population value (e.g., the sample mean).

Standard error (SE): How much your point estimate would vary if you drew many different samples of the same size. Smaller SE = more precise estimate. SE shrinks as sample size grows.

Confidence interval (CI): A range of plausible values for the population parameter, constructed so that (in repeated sampling) the specified percentage of intervals would contain the true value.

\[SE(\bar{x}) = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

\[95\% \text{ CI for } \mu \approx \bar{x} \pm 1.96 \times SE(\bar{x})\]

9.3.1 The Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states: regardless of the shape of the original distribution, the sampling distribution of the mean becomes approximately normal as sample size increases.

This is why t-tests and confidence intervals work even when individual measurements are not normally distributed ? as long as the sample size is large enough (roughly \(n \geq 30\) for mildly skewed data; larger for heavily skewed data).

9.4 Example 1: Clinical Data (PBC)

pbc <- survival::pbc %>%
  as_tibble() %>%
  janitor::clean_names() %>%
  mutate(
    sex   = factor(sex, levels = c("f", "m"), labels = c("Female", "Male")),
    trt   = factor(trt, levels = c(1, 2),     labels = c("D-penicillamine", "Placebo"))
  )

9.4.1 Estimating Mean Albumin

albumin_est <- pbc %>%
  filter(!is.na(albumin)) %>%
  summarise(
    n        = n(),
    mean     = mean(albumin),
    sd       = sd(albumin),
    se       = sd / sqrt(n),
    ci_lower = mean - qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se,
    ci_upper = mean + qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se
  )
albumin_est
# A tibble: 1 × 6
      n  mean    sd     se ci_lower ci_upper
  <int> <dbl> <dbl>  <dbl>    <dbl>    <dbl>
1   418  3.50 0.425 0.0208     3.46     3.54

Interpretation: We estimate the mean serum albumin to be 3.5 g/dL (95% CI: 3.46 to 3.54). If we were to repeat this study 100 times with different samples of the same size, approximately 95 of the resulting intervals would contain the true population mean.

9.4.3 Confidence Interval for a Proportion

What proportion of patients are female?

n_female <- sum(pbc$sex == "Female", na.rm = TRUE)
n_total  <- sum(!is.na(pbc$sex))

prop.test(x = n_female, n = n_total, conf.level = 0.95)

    1-sample proportions test with continuity correction

data:  n_female out of n_total, null probability 0.5
X-squared = 258.95, df = 1, p-value < 2.2e-16
alternative hypothesis: true p is not equal to 0.5
95 percent confidence interval:
 0.8603074 0.9216882
sample estimates:
        p 
0.8947368

The Wilson interval from prop.test() is better than the simple Wald interval for proportions, especially when the proportion is near 0 or 1.

9.5 Example 2: Animal Data (Mouse Weights)

Suppose we measure body weight in a small experiment: 20 mice in each of two treatment groups.

set.seed(2024)
mice <- tibble(
  group  = rep(c("Control", "Treatment"), each = 20),
  weight = c(rnorm(20, mean = 28, sd = 3),
             rnorm(20, mean = 25, sd = 3))
)

mice %>%
  group_by(group) %>%
  summarise(n = n(), mean = mean(weight), sd = sd(weight),
            se = sd / sqrt(n),
            ci_lower = mean - qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se,
            ci_upper = mean + qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se)
# A tibble: 2 × 7
  group         n  mean    sd    se ci_lower ci_upper
  <chr>     <int> <dbl> <dbl> <dbl>    <dbl>    <dbl>
1 Control      20  27.1  3.63 0.811     25.4     28.8
2 Treatment    20  24.7  2.64 0.591     23.5     25.9

9.5.1 Bootstrap Confidence Intervals

When the distribution is non-normal or the sample is small, bootstrap CIs are more reliable. The boot package resamples the data with replacement many times to build an empirical sampling distribution.

# Bootstrap 95% CI for mean weight in control mice
ctrl_weights <- mice$weight[mice$group == "Control"]

boot_mean <- boot(data  = ctrl_weights,
                  statistic = function(x, i) mean(x[i]),
                  R     = 1000)

boot.ci(boot_mean, type = "perc")
BOOTSTRAP CONFIDENCE INTERVAL CALCULATIONS
Based on 1000 bootstrap replicates

CALL : 
boot.ci(boot.out = boot_mean, type = "perc")

Intervals : 
Level     Percentile     
95%   (25.47, 28.59 )  
Calculations and Intervals on Original Scale

The percentile bootstrap CI takes the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile of the bootstrapped means as the interval bounds ? no distributional assumptions required.

9.7 What Can Go Wrong

Misinterpreting a confidence interval A 95% CI does not mean “there is a 95% probability the true mean lies in this interval.” The true mean either is or is not in the interval ? it is not random. The correct interpretation is: “if we repeated the study many times, 95% of such intervals would contain the true mean.”

Using a z-interval when n is small The formula \(\bar{x} \pm 1.96 \times SE\) uses the normal quantile 1.96. For small samples, use the t-distribution quantile: qt(0.975, df = n - 1). t.test() does this automatically.

Ignoring missing data If you compute mean(x) without na.rm = TRUE, R returns NA. Worse, if missing values are not random (e.g., sicker patients more likely to have missing labs), your estimate will be biased.

Treating overlapping CIs as proof of no difference Non-overlapping CIs do imply a significant difference (at approximately p < 0.05). But overlapping CIs do not prove no difference ? you need a proper two-sample test. The 95% CI for each group mean is not the same as the 95% CI for their difference.

9.8.1 Exercise 1 (Guided): CI for Bilirubin

Using survival::pbc:

  1. Compute the sample mean, SD, SE, and 95% CI for serum bilirubin (bili).
  2. Bilirubin is right-skewed. Repeat using log(bili). Report the geometric mean by back-transforming with exp().
  3. Compare the raw and geometric means. Which better represents the “typical” patient?
Exercise 1: Solution 
# Raw bilirubin
pbc %>%
  filter(!is.na(bili)) %>%
  summarise(
    n        = n(),
    mean     = mean(bili),
    se       = sd(bili) / sqrt(n),
    ci_lower = mean - qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se,
    ci_upper = mean + qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se
  )
# A tibble: 1 × 5
      n  mean    se ci_lower ci_upper
  <int> <dbl> <dbl>    <dbl>    <dbl>
1   418  3.22 0.216     2.80     3.64
# Log-transformed
log_est <- pbc %>%
  filter(!is.na(bili)) %>%
  summarise(
    n        = n(),
    log_mean = mean(log(bili)),
    se       = sd(log(bili)) / sqrt(n),
    ci_lower = log_mean - qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se,
    ci_upper = log_mean + qt(0.975, df = n - 1) * se
  )

# Back-transform to original scale
log_est %>%
  mutate(across(c(log_mean, ci_lower, ci_upper), exp))
# A tibble: 1 × 5
      n log_mean     se ci_lower ci_upper
  <int>    <dbl>  <dbl>    <dbl>    <dbl>
1   418     1.77 0.0501     1.60     1.95

The geometric mean is lower than the arithmetic mean, because the arithmetic mean is pulled upward by extreme values. The geometric mean better represents the median patient in a right-skewed distribution.

9.8.2 Exercise 2 (Semi-guided): Bootstrap vs t-interval

Using the mouse weight data created above (or simulate your own 15 observations from a skewed distribution):

  1. Compute the t-interval for the mean using the formula and verify it matches t.test().
  2. Compute a bootstrap percentile CI using boot().
  3. Are the two intervals similar? If the distribution were more skewed, which would you trust more?
Exercise 2: Solution 
set.seed(2024)
weights <- rnorm(15, mean = 28, sd = 4)

# t-interval
n <- length(weights)
m <- mean(weights); s <- sd(weights)
ci_t <- c(m - qt(0.975, df = n-1) * s/sqrt(n),
          m + qt(0.975, df = n-1) * s/sqrt(n))
ci_t
[1] 24.79043 30.29735
t.test(weights)$conf.int
[1] 24.79043 30.29735
attr(,"conf.level")
[1] 0.95
# bootstrap
library(boot)
b <- boot(weights, function(x, i) mean(x[i]), R = 2000)
boot.ci(b, type = "perc")$percent[4:5]
[1] 25.03384 29.76708

For normal data, both intervals are similar. For skewed data or small samples, the bootstrap is more reliable because it makes no normality assumption.

9.9 Comprehension Check

  1. What is the standard error of the mean, and how does it change as sample size increases?
  2. A study reports: “mean albumin 3.5 g/dL (95% CI: 3.3 to 3.7).” What does this mean?
  3. Why does the Central Limit Theorem matter for confidence intervals even when the data are not normally distributed?
  4. You have a sample of 10 mice with a skewed weight distribution. Should you use a t-interval or a bootstrap CI? Why?
  5. Two groups have CIs of [10.0, 12.0] and [11.5, 13.5]. They overlap at [11.5, 12.0]. Can you conclude there is no significant difference between the groups?
Answers
  1. The standard error is \(SE = s / \sqrt{n}\), where \(s\) is the sample standard deviation. It measures how precisely the sample mean estimates the population mean. SE decreases as \(n\) increases (as \(1/\sqrt{n}\)) — doubling the sample size reduces the SE by a factor of \(\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\).
  2. Our best estimate of the mean population albumin is 3.5 g/dL. If we repeated this study many times with samples of the same size, 95% of the resulting confidence intervals would contain the true population mean.
  3. The CLT guarantees that for sufficiently large samples, the sampling distribution of the mean is approximately normal regardless of the original distribution shape. This allows us to use the normal (or t) distribution to construct valid CIs even for skewed data.
  4. With only 10 mice and skewed data, the normality assumption underlying the t-interval is suspect. Use a bootstrap CI, which makes no distributional assumption.
  5. No. Overlapping CIs do not imply no significant difference. The CI for each group mean is not the CI for their difference. You need a two-sample t-test (or equivalent) to formally test whether the groups differ.
